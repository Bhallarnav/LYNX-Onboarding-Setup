Module 4.4 — Offers & Negotiation on the Listing Side

An offer on the table is the moment everything you've done as a listing agent gets tested. How you handle the next few hours determines whether your seller walks away feeling like they got the best possible outcome — or not.

🚨 Call a mentor before you touch paperwork you're unsure about If an offer comes in and any part of it is unclear — the language, a clause, what a condition actually means, how to structure a counter — call Mitch, Kyle, Arnav, or Chetan before you respond. We are a team. Getting the paperwork right matters more than responding fast. Speed without accuracy in an offer situation can cost your seller money or create legal exposure. Call first.

What to Do When an Offer Comes In — First Steps​

The moment you receive an offer notification, a clock starts — both for the irrevocable deadline on the offer and for your seller's emotional state. Move quickly and deliberately.

  1. Acknowledge receipt immediately. Reply to the buyer's agent within minutes to confirm you have the offer and will present it to the seller. Note the irrevocable time and date — that is your hard deadline.
  2. Read the offer fully before you call the seller. Understand every term before you pick up the phone. You cannot walk a seller through an offer you haven't read yourself. Take 10 minutes. Know what's in it.
  3. Call the seller — don't text. This is a significant moment in their life. It deserves a phone call. Tell them an offer has come in, give them the headline (price, conditions, possession), and set a time to review it together.
  4. Send the offer to the seller before your meeting. Give them time to read it themselves so the review conversation is a discussion, not a first read. Send with a note: "Take a look — we'll go through it together. Don't sign anything until we talk."
  5. If you cannot be there — get help immediately. If you're unavailable to present the offer before the irrevocable expires, contact Mitch or another team member right away. Never let an irrevocable deadline pass without a response because you couldn't get there. The team will cover you — but only if you ask in time.

⚠️ The irrevocable deadline is not a suggestion Once the irrevocable time passes, the buyer is no longer bound to the offer. If you miss it — even by an hour — the deal can evaporate. Know the deadline the moment the offer arrives. Set a reminder. Plan your seller meeting accordingly.

Reviewing the Offer With Your Seller — Without Editorializing​

Your job when reviewing an offer is to make sure the seller understands every term — not to tell them how to feel about it. Present the offer neutrally, explain the terms clearly, and then answer their questions. Save your strategic advice for after the review, not during it.

TermHow to Present It
Purchase priceState the number plainly. Don't add "which is below asking" or "which is a strong offer" until after the full review. Let them hear the whole picture first.
DepositHow much, when it's due, and what happens to it if the deal falls through. Sellers often don't understand that a larger deposit signals buyer commitment.
ConditionsExplain each condition clearly: financing, inspection, and any other. What it means, how long they have, and what happens if it's not removed. This is where most sellers need the most help.
Possession dateDoes it work for the seller's timeline? Flag immediately if it conflicts with where they're going.
Inclusions / exclusionsDoes the offer include items the seller was planning to take? Does it exclude items the seller expected to leave? Resolve all ambiguity here.
IrrevocableThe deadline the buyer has set. The seller must respond — accept, counter, or reject — before this time. Make sure they understand the urgency without creating panic.

After the full review — transitioning to strategy:

"Okay — now that you've heard everything, I want to give you my honest read on this offer and what I think your options are. Then the decision is completely yours."

This is the pivot from neutral review to strategic advice. Do it explicitly — it signals that what comes next is your professional opinion, not just more information.

Multiple Offers — How the Process Works and How to Advise the Seller​

Multiple offers are a good problem — but they create real complexity and real risk of mishandling. Your obligation is to treat every buyer fairly and advise your seller clearly on their options.

StageWhat to Know
Disclose existenceYou are required to disclose to all parties that multiple offers exist — but not the terms of those offers. Every buyer's agent must be told there are competing offers before their client submits.
Seller's optionsThe seller can: (1) review all offers and accept the best one outright, (2) counter one offer while declining the others, or (3) invite all buyers to submit their best and final offer by a set deadline. Walk them through all three before they decide.
Best & finalIf the seller requests best and final, set a clear deadline and communicate it to all agents simultaneously. Be precise — the time, the date, the format required. Do not give any buyer an advantage through timing or information asymmetry.
Evaluating offersPrice is not always the strongest offer. Fewer conditions, a larger deposit, a flexible possession, or a clean offer from a well-qualified buyer may be worth more than an extra $5,000 from a buyer with three conditions and a shaky pre-approval.
DocumentationKeep a record of every offer received, when it was presented to the seller, and their response. This protects you, the seller, and the integrity of the process. Ask Mitch how the team documents multiple offer situations.

📋 Call Mitch before running your first multiple offer situation The disclosure obligations, timing, and documentation requirements in a multiple offer scenario are not something to navigate alone the first time. The moment you have a second offer coming in, call Mitch. Walk through it together.

Countering — Language, Strategy, What's Worth Countering vs. Accepting​

A counter offer is a rejection of the original offer and a new offer back. Once you counter, the original offer is gone. The buyer is not obligated to accept your counter — they can walk. Know what you're giving up before you issue one.

Worth countering on:

  • Price that's meaningfully below what the market supports — not just below asking
  • Possession date that doesn't work for the seller's timeline — and where the buyer may have flexibility
  • Condition periods that are unreasonably long when a shorter window is standard
  • Inclusions that were never meant to be part of the deal

Consider accepting instead:

  • A clean offer at or near market value — countering for a small increment risks losing the deal for the wrong reason
  • An offer where the buyer has already made a significant concession — pushing further strains goodwill
  • A strong offer in a slow or softening market — the next offer may not be better
  • Any situation where the seller's primary goal is certainty, not maximum price

Framing a counter to the seller:

"Here's what I'd recommend countering on and why — and here's what I think is worth letting go. Ultimately, a counter is a rejection of their offer, so we want to be strategic about what we're asking for. What's the one thing that matters most to you on this?"

Anchor the seller to one priority. Countering on five things at once signals desperation, not strength. Pick the most important term and counter cleanly.

⚠️ Counter language matters — don't improvise it Counter offer language must be precise. Use the standard AREA counter offer form — do not write amendments in free text or email and assume they're binding. If you're unsure about the right form or wording, ask Mitch before you send anything to the buyer's agent.

How to Advise a Seller on a Low Offer Without Making the Decision for Them​

A low offer is emotionally triggering for most sellers. Your job is to be the calm one in the room — to acknowledge their reaction, reframe the situation strategically, and present their options clearly. Never dismiss a low offer without considering it.

When the seller's first reaction is to reject outright:

"I completely understand — it's not the number you were hoping for. Before we reject it, let me ask: what would change about this offer that would make it worth working with? Because sometimes a low price offer has other strong terms, and a counter gets us to a deal we're actually happy with. What would it need to look like for you to say yes?"

This question redirects the seller from emotion to criteria. Once you know what they need, you can evaluate whether the gap is closeable.

  • Present the net proceeds from the low offer alongside your CMA number — sometimes the gap is smaller than it feels
  • If the offer is truly below market with no redeeming terms, you can say so — clearly and calmly — but the decision to reject is still theirs
  • A rejected offer with no counter closes the door. A counter keeps the conversation going. Default to countering unless the seller has a specific reason not to.
  • If the seller wants to reject without countering, document it and move on

When to Advise Accepting vs. Holding Out​

The accept vs. hold-out decision depends on two things: the strength of the current offer relative to market, and the realistic probability that a better offer is coming. You cannot guarantee either. Be honest about that when you advise.

  • If the offer is at or above market value with clean terms — advise accepting. The risk of holding out for more is rarely worth it unless you have strong evidence a better buyer is imminent.
  • If you have multiple showings booked, strong market activity, or another agent who has signalled interest — a short hold may be warranted. Be specific: "Let's hold until [date/time] and see if the other interest materialises."
  • If the market is soft, days on market are increasing, or this is the first offer after a long stretch of no activity — advise carefully. A bird in hand is real. The next offer is hypothetical.
  • Never promise that a better offer is coming. You don't know that. Present probabilities, not certainties.
  • When in doubt, call Mitch. The hold-out decision is where inexperienced agents most often give sellers bad advice with good intentions.

Framing the accept vs. hold-out conversation:

"Here's my honest read: this offer is [assessment relative to market]. If we decline or let it expire, I think the probability of getting something better is [high / moderate / uncertain], and here's why. But I want to be clear — I can't guarantee the next offer is better. What I can tell you is what the data says and what my gut says. The decision is yours."

When You're Not Available to Review With Your Client​

If you cannot present an offer before the irrevocable expires, call Mitch, Kyle, Arnav, or Chetan immediately. We are a team. Ask them to step in and handle the presentation on your behalf. Brief them fully on the seller — their priorities, the listing history, any context — so they can advise properly. This is not a failure. Leaving an irrevocable deadline to expire because you couldn't find coverage is.

What "Firm" Really Means Once Conditions Are Removed​

A firm deal is a deal where all conditions have been waived or satisfied and both parties are unconditionally committed. This is a significant legal moment and sellers often don't fully understand what it means.

  • Once a deal is firm, the seller cannot accept another offer or back out without significant legal consequence
  • The buyer cannot walk away without forfeiting their deposit and potentially facing legal action
  • Any changes to terms after firm status — possession date, inclusions, anything — require a written amendment signed by both parties
  • Verbally agreeing to a change after conditions are removed means nothing legally — everything must be in writing
  • The deposit is typically due within a short window after conditions are removed — confirm the timeline and follow up to ensure it's received

✅ What to say to the seller when the deal goes firm Call them. Don't just text. Acknowledge what just happened — this is a real milestone in their life. Confirm the key dates: deposit due date, condition removal date on file, possession date. Tell them what happens next. Then let them celebrate.

Common Mistakes Listing Agents Make When an Offer Is on the Table​

  • Editorializing during the offer review. Saying "that's a really low offer" or "this is a strong one" before the seller has formed their own view colours their judgment. Present first. Advise after.
  • Missing the irrevocable deadline. There is no excuse for this. If you can't be there in time, get a team member to cover. The irrevocable is sacred.
  • Countering on too many terms at once. A counter with five changes looks desperate and gives the buyer too many exit points. Pick the one or two things that matter most.
  • Making the decision for the seller. Saying "I wouldn't accept this if I were you" is the line. Present options and consequences — let them decide.
  • Verbally agreeing to terms without documentation. Any change to an accepted offer must be in writing. A text or call agreeing to a different possession date is not binding and will cause problems at closing.
  • Forgetting to confirm condition removal. When a condition removal deadline passes, follow up. If the buyer hasn't removed their condition and you haven't heard, call the buyer's agent immediately. Don't assume.
  • Going quiet after the offer is accepted. The seller's anxiety doesn't end at acceptance — it peaks during the condition period. Call them after the inspection. Call them when financing is approved. Keep the communication alive.

Dealing With the Opposition — Negotiating With the Buyer's Agent​

The buyer's agent is not your enemy — but they are not on your side. They have a fiduciary duty to their client, and so do you. The best deals happen when both agents are professional, communicate clearly, and focus on solving problems rather than winning arguments.

When they make a low offer with aggressive justification

Their move: "My clients feel the home is overpriced based on [comp]. We're at [number] and that's where we need to be."

Your response: "I appreciate you sharing that. Our pricing is based on [specific comps]. I'm going to present this to my seller and come back to you. What flexibility does your client have if we counter?"

When they pressure you on the irrevocable timeline

Their move: "My clients need an answer in the next hour or they're moving on."

Your response: "I understand. I'm presenting to my seller now and will get back to you as quickly as I can. If there's flexibility in that window, it helps — but I'll do my best to meet it."

When they push back hard on your counter

Their move: "We've already moved significantly. My client is at their limit."

Your response: "Noted — I'll bring that back to my seller. What would make the deal work for your client on [the one term you're still apart on]? Sometimes there's a creative solution."

When they try to negotiate after conditions are removed

Their move: "The inspector found a few things — my client wants a price adjustment."

Your response: "I'll bring that to my seller. What I'd say is that the inspection period was the time for this conversation — we'll see what my seller's appetite is, but they're under no obligation to adjust. Can you send me the specific items in writing?"

💡 Tone is a negotiating tool The agent who stays calm, professional, and solution-oriented almost always has more leverage than the one who escalates. Buyers' agents talk to each other. A reputation for being difficult to work with affects future deals — yours and your seller's. Be firm on substance. Be easy to deal with on everything else.
