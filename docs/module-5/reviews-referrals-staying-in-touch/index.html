5.5 Reviews, Referrals & Staying in Touch

Closing the deal is the beginning of the relationship, not the end of it. The clients who feel taken care of after the transaction are the ones who send you business for the next ten years.

When to Ask for a Google Review — Timing Is Everything​

The window for a Google review is shorter than most agents think. Ask too early — before possession, while they're still stressed — and you'll get a lukewarm response or nothing at all. Ask too late — three months after closing — and the emotional high has faded and it feels like an admin task.

TimingWhy It Doesn't Work
Too early — before or during the dealThey're anxious. They haven't experienced the outcome yet. Don't ask.
Too late — more than 3–4 weeks post-closingThe excitement has cooled. You'll get a yes but the follow-through rate drops sharply.
The sweet spot — The second conditions are waivedThey're in the deal is firm, the stress is gone, and the gratitude is at its peak. This is when to ask.

✅ Set a FUB reminder the day before possession The moment keys are handed over, set a task in FUB for Day 3 and Day 7. Day 3 is your check-in text. Day 7 is when you ask how their first week has been going. Don't rely on memory — set the task.

Phone — The moment the final waiver is signed:​

"CONGRATULATIONS! I have a small favour to ask. A Google review means the world for someone like me who's building their business on referrals. It doesn't have to be long — even just a sentence or two about your experience would be huge. I'll text you the link right now so you have it."

Send the link immediately after hanging up. Don't wait. The longer the gap, the less likely they follow through.

Text — if you didn't get a chance to call:

"Hey [Name]! I have a small favour — if you have 2 minutes, a Google review would genuinely mean a lot to me and helps other people find me. Here's the link: [link]. No pressure at all, and thank you again for trusting me with this one — it was a real pleasure."

If they don't respond within 5 days, a single gentle follow-up is fine: "Hey — just wanted to make sure that link worked for you!" After that, let it go.

⚠️ Never ask more than twice One ask plus one follow-up is the limit. Pushing harder than that damages the relationship — and the relationship is worth more than any review. If they don't do it, move on and stay warm.

The Referral Ask — How to Make It Natural, Not Transactional​

The referral ask is not a pitch. It's a reminder — said simply and genuinely — that you're in this business and you love what you do. The best referral asks don't feel like asks at all. They feel like a natural part of the conversation.

There are two moments when referrals come up most naturally: right after a great experience, and when someone's life is changing. Keep your ears open for both.

At the end of the review conversation:

"And honestly — the biggest compliment you could give me is just keeping me in mind if anyone you know mentions they're thinking about buying or selling. I love working with people who come from someone I already know."

Say this once. Don't repeat it. It plants the seed without pressure.

In an ongoing relationship — when it comes up naturally:

"By the way — I'm always looking to help good people. If you ever hear of anyone thinking about making a move, even just thinking about it, tell them to call me. I'll take good care of them."

This works on annual calls, at social events, whenever. It's light, genuine, and easy to say.

📋 When someone sends you a referral Call them the same day to say thank you. Then call them again after you've met the referral to close the loop. Then call them when the deal closes. Three touches for one referral — that's how you get a second one.

Post-Closing Follow-Up Sequence​

Most agents disappear after closing. The ones who stay in touch get the repeat business and the referrals. This sequence is four touchpoints over twelve months — then it becomes part of your annual sphere rhythm.

Day 7 — The check-in text

A warm, genuine text to see how they're settling in. This is also when you plant the review ask if the tone is right. Keep it short — you're a person checking in, not an agent following up.

"Hey [Name]! It's been a week — how are you settling into the new place? Hope the move went smoothly and you're starting to feel at home."

Day 30 — The "how's the home?" call

This one is a phone call, not a text. Call to ask how the first month has been. Ask if anything came up they didn't expect. Ask if they need any contractor or service recommendations. This call is about being useful, not selling anything.

"Hey [Name], it's [Your Name] — just wanted to check in now that you've had a full month in the place. How's everything going? Any surprises, good or bad?"

Month 6 — Market update touchpoint

A text or brief email with something genuinely useful about the market — what's happened in their neighbourhood, what their home might be worth now, what the market is doing. Make it personal to their property, not a mass email.

"Hey [Name]! Quick market update — homes in [neighbourhood] have moved quite a bit since you closed. Wanted to make sure you're in the loop. Happy to chat if you're curious what yours looks like now."

Month 12 — The anniversary call

Call on or near the anniversary of their possession date. Acknowledge the milestone, give them a market update specific to their home, and make the referral ask naturally. This call, done every year, is worth more than almost any other marketing you'll do.

"Hey [Name]! Can you believe it's already been a year? I was thinking about you. How's everything going with the place? I pulled some numbers for your neighbourhood — your home has done really well. Want me to send you a quick summary?"

💡 After month 12 — fold them into your sphere rhythm Once you've completed the first-year sequence, move them into your A or B sphere tier and treat them like any other high-value contact: 2–3 meaningful touches per month for A-tier, monthly for B. They bought a home with you. That relationship is worth protecting.

How to Build a Database That Sends You Referrals for Years​

A referral database isn't a spreadsheet — it's a set of relationships you've intentionally maintained. The difference between an agent who gets one referral per year and one who gets ten is almost always consistency. Not charm. Not luck. Consistency.

CategoryHow to Treat Them
Every past clientStaged in FUB as "Past Client" On your annual anniversary sequence. In your sphere rhythm after year one. Never let a past client go cold.
Referral partnersMortgage brokers, lawyers, home inspectors, stagers, cleaners — anyone you refer business to should be in your database. They send you business when you send them business.
Warm referrersPeople who've already referred you once. These contacts get extra attention — they've proven they'll advocate for you. Tag them separately and thank them specifically and visibly when they send someone your way.
Life change flagsWhen someone in your database gets engaged, has a baby, changes jobs, or gets divorced — flag it. These are real estate triggers. A personal, timely note in that moment is worth 12 months of market updates.
Annual reviewOnce a year, go through your database and re-tier everyone. Contacts who've gone cold move down. People who've been engaging move up. Your A-tier should feel like a real shortlist of your best relationships.

✅ The referral compound effect One client who buys with you, gets great service, and refers two people — who each refer two more — creates a pipeline that grows without any paid advertising. This is not theory. It's how the top agents on every team built their book. Start your database right and maintain it every single year.

Post-Closing Email and Text Templates​

Email — Post-possession thank you (send within 24 hours of key handover)

Subject: Congratulations on your new home — and thank you

Hi [Name],

I just wanted to take a moment to say how much it meant to be part of this. Helping someone find — or sell — a home is one of those things I genuinely love about this work, and getting to do it with people like you makes it even better.

I hope the move goes smoothly and you feel completely at home as soon as you walk in the door. I'll check in with you in a week to see how you're settling in.

In the meantime, if anything comes up — a question about the property, a contractor recommendation, anything at all — don't hesitate to reach out. You've got my number.

Congratulations again. Truly.

[Your Name]

Keep this warm and personal. Don't include the review ask here — too soon, and it undercuts the sincerity of the note.

Text — Day 7 check-in

"Hey [Name]! It's been a week — how are you settling into the new place? Hope the move went smoothly and you're starting to feel at home. Anything come up I can help with?"

If the reply is warm and positive, this is a natural moment to transition into the review ask.

Text — Month 6 market update

"Hey [Name]! Hard to believe it's been six months. Wanted to share a quick market update — [neighbourhood] has been [moving / holding steady / seeing some interesting activity] and your home has done well since you closed. Happy to pull together a quick summary if you're curious what the numbers look like now. Hope you're loving it over there!"

Personalize with an actual neighbourhood stat. A generic market text feels like a mass message — one specific detail makes it feel personal.

Phone call — Month 12 anniversary call (what to cover)

Open: "Hey [Name]! Can you believe it's already been a year since you got the keys?"

Check in: "How's everything going with the place? Anything you've done to it since you moved in?"

Market update: "I pulled some numbers for your area — your home has actually done [really well / held its value / appreciated nicely]. Want me to send you a quick summary?"

Referral: "I'm still growing my business on referrals — if you ever hear of anyone thinking about making a move, I'd love the introduction."

Close: "It was honestly one of my favourite transactions. Thanks again for trusting me with it."

This call should feel like catching up with someone you like, not a sales call. The market update and referral ask come naturally — they're not the point of the call, they're part of it.
