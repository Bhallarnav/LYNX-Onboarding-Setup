5.4 Open Houses

An open house isn't just a favour to the seller. Done right, it's one of the highest-leverage lead generation activities you have. Two hours on a Sunday can change your pipeline for the month.

How to Choose Which Open House to Run​

Not all open houses are created equal. Where you run one — and whose listing it is — changes what you're optimizing for. Know your goal before you show up.

TypeWhen to Run It
Your listingBest for seller relationship and your personal brand. Every buyer who walks in is a potential client. Run these every time — no exceptions.
Team listingGreat for lead gen in neighbourhoods where you want to build presence. Get permission from the listing agent first — always. Split any leads with the listing agent as agreed.
Brokerage listingGood early-career practice and lead gen when your own pipeline is thin. Confirm the protocol with Mitch first to avoid any mistakes.

📋 How to pick the right property Choose a home that's priced well and shows well — curb appeal matters because walk-in traffic depends on it. High-traffic streets, popular neighbourhoods, and recently reduced listings all draw more visitors. A home that's been sitting is harder to generate energy around. Apartments have hard access so bad foot traffic.

Setup — Signs, Materials, FUB Prep, and Registration​

The hour before the open house is as important as the open house itself. Show up 15-30 minutes early. If you're scrambling when the first person walks in, they'll feel it.

Signs & exterior​

  • 3–6 directional signs placed at key intersections leading to the property — start from the nearest major road
  • Open house sign at the front of the property with your name, time, and contact info visible
  • Balloons or flags if allowed by the seller — anything that makes the home easy to spot
  • Front door unlocked, lights on, curtains open before the first visitor arrives

Materials & interior​

  • Feature sheets printed — enough for expected traffic plus 20% buffer
  • Your business cards at the entrance — never run out
  • Sign-in sheet or tablet set up at the door — physical and/or digital
  • Any seller-requested items addressed (remove personal photos if asked, put away valuables)
  • Music on at low volume, temperature comfortable, any odours addressed
  • Water or light refreshments if appropriate for the price point

FUB prep (night before)​

  • Create a new "Open House" task in FUB with the property address and date
  • Set up a new contact tag: "OH — [Property Address] — [Date]" for all visitors you'll add
  • Prepare your follow-up action plan so you can assign it to new contacts same-day
  • Have FUB open on your phone during the open house — log contacts as they come in if possible

The Greeting — "Welcome to the Party"​

The way you greet someone sets the entire tone of the visit. People decide in the first few seconds whether you're someone they want to talk to — or someone they're going to avoid for the next 20 minutes while they look at the kitchen.

Don't stand at the door with a clipboard. Don't block the entrance. Don't immediately ask for their information. Let them in, make them feel welcome, and give them a second to land.

The opening — say this every time:

"Hey, welcome! Come on in — make yourself at home. Take a look around, and I'll give you a quick two-minute tour whenever you're ready. I'm [Your Name], by the way."

Then step back. Don't follow them immediately. Let them breathe. You'll get your moment.

⚠️ What not to say "Have you signed in?" before they've even walked in the door. "Are you working with an agent?" as your second sentence. "What's your budget?" within the first five minutes. All of these feel like interrogation, not hospitality. You'll get the information — just not like that.

How to Have a Real Conversation​

Most agents either hover awkwardly or disappear into the corner. Neither works. Your job is to be a good host — interested, relaxed, and genuinely curious about who walked in the door.

After they've had 3–5 minutes to look around, approach naturally. The goal of the first conversation is not to qualify them. It's to be someone they like talking to.

Opening a real conversation:

"What do you think so far? Does this feel like the right size, or are you looking for something different?"

This is a real question, not a script. Listen to the answer. Their response tells you everything: are they actively looking, are they a neighbour satisfying curiosity, are they already working with an agent, do they have a timeline?

Moving toward the important questions — naturally:

"Are you pretty early in the search, or do you have a sense of timeline yet?"

"Have you had a chance to connect with a mortgage broker yet, or is that still on the list?"

"This neighbourhood has been busy lately — have you been watching the market here for a while?"

These feel like conversation, not qualification. But they tell you: urgency, financing status, and engagement level. Ask one at a time. Don't run through all three back-to-back.

  • If they're not interested in talking, don't push — give them space and let them come to you
  • If they're a neighbour just being nosy, be friendly. They know 10 people who might be buying or selling
  • If they mention they're already working with an agent, be warm and helpful — don't disengage
  • If they ask a question you don't know the answer to, say "good question — let me find that out for you" and actually follow up

Handling "Just Looking"​

"Just looking" is not a rejection. It's a reflex. Almost everyone says it. Almost everyone is actually looking for something specific — they just don't want to be sold to before they've had a chance to breathe.

When they say "just looking":

"Perfect — that's exactly what today is for. Take your time. I'll be around if you have any questions about the place or the neighbourhood."

Then let them look. Don't trail them. When they come back to ask something — and they will — that's your real opening.

When they come back to you:

"So what did you think — anything catch your eye, or is it missing something you're looking for?"

This question works whether they loved it or hated it. Either answer moves the conversation forward. If they loved it, talk timing. If it missed the mark, ask what they are looking for — and offer to send them listings that fit.

✅ The sign-in ask — do it after, not before Wait until you've had a real conversation before asking for contact info. Then make it natural: "I'd love to send you a couple of listings that might be closer to what you're looking for — what's the best way to reach you?" Almost nobody says no to that.

The Follow-Up — What to Send, When, and How to Log It​

Follow up the same day. Not tomorrow. Not Monday if it was Sunday. The same day, within 2–3 hours of the open house ending. Your notes are freshest, their memory of the conversation is freshest, and the speed signals professionalism.

TimingAction
Same daySend your first follow-up text to every contact
24–48 hoursSend listings or market info to anyone who asked
Day 3–5Second touch for hot and warm leads — call if warranted
Before bedAll contacts added, tagged, and on an action plan in FUB

Hot lead — actively looking, motivated, ready to move

"Hey [Name], it was great meeting you today at [address]! Based on what you mentioned, I think I've already got a couple of listings in mind that might be a better fit. Would you be open to a quick call this week so I can send you some options? Happy to work around your schedule."

Follow up with a call within 48 hours if they don't respond to the text. Hot 1-3 months Stage.

Warm lead — interested but no clear timeline yet

"Hey [Name], thanks for coming by today — great to meet you! I'll keep an eye out for properties that match what you're looking for and send them your way as they come up. In the meantime, feel free to reach out anytime if you have questions about the market or anything else. No pressure at all."

Add to a 3-6 months warm Stage in FUB. Touch once a month until they're ready.

Cold / neighbour — just curious, no intent to buy or sell soon

"Hey [Name], thanks so much for stopping by today! It was great to meet a neighbour. If you're ever curious about what's happening in the market around here — or if you know anyone thinking about making a move — don't hesitate to reach out. Happy to help anytime."

Add to your sphere database. They live on your turf — stay warm. Neighbours refer. Cold Stage 6+ months.

How to Debrief the Open House​

Every open house should teach you something. Log the debrief in FUB as a note on the property contact or in your own tracking doc. Over time, this data tells you which properties pull traffic, which neighbourhoods to focus on, and where your conversion is happening.

What to record after every open house​

  • Total visitors (number of groups or individuals who showed up)
  • Number of contacts captured (signed in with real info)
  • Hot / warm / cold breakdown — how many of each - What information did they tell you about their home search?
  • Any buyer leads who are actively in the market
  • Any seller leads — neighbours who mentioned thinking about listing
  • Feedback on the property (price concerns, layout issues, condition) — report this to the listing agent

💡 Share your debrief with Mitch After your first few open houses, bring the debrief numbers to your next check-in. Mitch can help you interpret the data and coach you on conversion. An open house with 20 visitors and 0 follow-ups is a missed opportunity — the debrief is where you figure out why.
