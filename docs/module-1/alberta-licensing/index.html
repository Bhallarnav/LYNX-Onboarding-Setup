1.4 Alberta Licensing in Plain English

note

Your licensing course taught you the rules. This section tells you what those rules actually mean when you're sitting across from a client. When in doubt on anything compliance-related — call your Mentor. Never guess.

RECA — what it actually means day-to-day​

note

RECA is Alberta's real estate regulator. They set the rules you operate under. You don't need to memorize the Act — you need to know the handful of things that come up on every deal.

  • The 4 things RECA cares about most in your daily work

    1. You must represent your client's best interests — not your own, not the other side's. Every piece of advice you give should be in their best interest. If it isn't, you have a problem.

    2. You must disclose your role clearly — clients need to know who you're working for before you have any real estate conversation with them. This is why we use representation agreements early.

    3. You must keep client information confidential — what your buyer can afford, why your seller is moving, any personal detail — stays between you and your client. Never share it with the other side.

    4. You must be honest and not mislead anyone — this includes buyers, sellers, other agents, and RECA itself. If you don't know something, say so. Don't guess and present it as fact.

Buyer representation — the practical version​

note

A buyer rep agreement makes you the client's agent. Without it, you're just a facilitator. We use them on every buyer relationship at Lynx.

  • When do we use it?

    Before you show property. Ideally at the buyer consultation. If a buyer wants to see a home before signing, have an honest conversation: explain that the agreement protects them, not just you, and that it defines the relationship clearly before money is on the table if the client still is hesitant to sign, don’t push, call your mentor.

  • How do you explain it without making it awkward?

    Say this: "Before we head out, I want to get a quick agreement signed — it just confirms that I'm working for you and your interests on this search. It protects you and makes sure everything is clear from the start. It takes two minutes. This agreement while for 3/6 months will be terminated at any time you wish without and penalties financial or otherwise."

    Keep it casual. The agents who fumble this make it feel like a big deal. It isn't.

Seller representation — what you owe your seller​

note

When you list a property, you are a fiduciary for that seller. That's a legal word that means you are legally obligated to act in their best interest. Take it seriously.

  • Your key duties to a seller

    • Promote and protect their interests above all others
    • Present every offer — even ones you think they won't accept
    • Keep their motivation, timeline, and bottom line confidential
    • Give them your honest opinion on price — even when it's not what they want to hear
    • Follow their lawful instructions, even if you disagree with their decision

  • What "promote their interests" looks like in practice

    It means you don't tell the buyer's agent your seller is going through a divorce and needs to sell fast. It means you don't hint that your seller will take less. It means when you're negotiating, you're in their corner — not playing both sides to get a deal done.

Disclosure — what you must tell people​

note

Disclosure errors are one of the most common ways agents get in trouble with RECA. When something feels like it should be disclosed — disclose it. When you're unsure — call your Mentor before the Offer To Purchase (OTP) closes, not after.

  • Latent defects — must disclose

    A latent defect is a hidden problem with the property that a buyer couldn't find through a normal inspection — foundation issues, past flooding, mold behind walls, structural problems. If you know about it, you must disclose it. Full stop. There is no scenario where staying quiet on a known latent defect is acceptable.

  • Patent defects — buyer's responsibility

    A patent defect is something visible and obvious — a cracked wall, a missing railing, staining on the ceiling. Buyers are expected to notice these themselves. You don't need to list every visible flaw, but you also should never actively hide them or steer buyers away from looking.

  • Material facts — always disclose

    A material fact is anything a reasonable person would want to know before buying. Examples: the property was a grow-op, a death occurred on the property within a certain timeframe, there are known easements or encroachments, there's a pending special levy on a condo. If you're unsure whether something is material — it probably is. Disclose it.

  • The "as is" clause — what it does and doesn't do

    Sellers sometimes want to sell "as is." This does not eliminate your disclosure obligations. You still must disclose known latent defects. "As is" means the seller isn't fixing anything — it doesn't mean they can hide problems. This trips up a lot of new agents.

Dual agency — what it is and why we're careful​

  • What is dual agency?

    Dual agency is when one agent (or one brokerage) represents both the buyer and the seller in the same transaction. In Alberta, this is permitted but heavily regulated. Both parties must give informed written consent, and your ability to advise either side is significantly limited.

  • What to do if it comes up

    Call Mitch immediately. Do not proceed with a dual agency situation on your own as a new agent. This is a situation that requires experience and careful handling. Don't guess, don't improvise.

The 60-second compliance checklist​

note

Run through this mentally on every new client relationship:

☐ Have I explained my role and who I represent?

☐ Is there a signed representation agreement in place?

☐ Have I disclosed anything I'm required to disclose?

☐ Am I acting in my client's best interest — or my own?

☐ Is there anything about this situation I should run by Mitch?

If you can answer yes to the first four and no to the last one — you're good. If you're unsure on any of them — stop and call.
