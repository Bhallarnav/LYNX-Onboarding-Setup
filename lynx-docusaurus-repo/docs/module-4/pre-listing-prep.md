You win the listing before you walk in the door. The preparation you do in the 24 hours before a seller appointment is what separates an agent who gets hired from one who gets thanked and shown out.

---

## Research Every Agent Must Do Before Walking in the Door

Walk into every listing appointment knowing more about the seller's home and neighbourhood than they do. This isn't about memorizing numbers — it's about earning the right to have a confident pricing conversation. Sellers can feel the difference between an agent who did the work and one who printed a report on the way over.

| Research Area | What to Find Out |
| --- | --- |
| The property itself | Pull the full MLS history — every time it's been listed, every price change, how long it sat, what it sold for. If it's been listed before and didn't sell, find out why. The seller may not tell you — the data will. |
| The neighbourhood | Know the last 6 months of sales in the immediate area — what sold, what didn't, what price range is moving. Be able to speak to the micro-market, not just Calgary overall. Sellers don't care about the city — they care about their street. |
| The seller's motivation | Before you arrive, find out as much as you can: why are they selling, where are they going, what's their timeline? This context changes everything — a seller who needs to close in 45 days thinks about price differently than someone with no urgency. |
| The competition | Who else might they be talking to? If you know another agent in the area is aggressive on price or commission, prepare for that conversation. Don't bring it up first — but don't be caught flat-footed either. |
| Active listings nearby | Know exactly what's currently for sale within a half-kilometre and at a similar price point. These are the homes your seller will be competing with. Know them well enough to explain how this property is better — or where it needs to be priced to win. |

> ⚠️ **Never walk in without doing the MLS pull**
Going into a listing appointment without a completed CMA is like showing up to a job interview without a resume. If you're short on time, do a quick CMA over a thorough one — but always have something in your hand. Call Mitch if you're unsure how to interpret the data before you go in.

## How to Build a CMA in MLS — Active Comps, Sold Comps, Expired Listings

A CMA (Comparative Market Analysis) is not a price guess — it's a structured argument for a specific number, supported by evidence. Every section of the CMA tells a different part of the story. Know what you're reading before you present it.

| Comp Type | What It Tells You |
| --- | --- |
| Active comps — your competition | What buyers are choosing between right now. Active listings set the ceiling — if your seller prices above everything else on the market, buyers won't come. Pull homes within the same property type, bedroom count, and rough square footage within 0.5–1km. |
| Sold comps — what the market actually paid | The most important number. Sold comps from the last 90 days (ideally 60) show what buyers actually paid — not what sellers hoped for. Appraisers and lenders use sold comps. This is your anchor for the pricing conversation. |
| Expired listings — the price ceiling warning | Homes that listed and didn't sell. These tell you where the market said no. When a seller pushes for a number above your CMA, showing them expired listings at that price range is your most powerful tool. The market already tested that price. It failed. |

> 📋 **How to pull a CMA in MLS — step by step**
Log into MLS Matrix. Run a search with the subject property's key attributes: property type, bedroom count, approximate square footage (±20%), and a radius of 0.5–1km from the address. Filter for: (1) Active — last 90 days, (2) Sold — last 90 days, (3) Expired — last 90 days. Export all three as a report. Then manually review each comp and exclude any that are genuinely incomparable — different lot size, major renovations, backing onto a busy road. Ask Kyle to walk you through your first pull if you haven't done one before.

## Understanding List-to-Sale Ratios and Days on Market

Two numbers matter more than almost anything else in a listing conversation: the list-to-sale ratio and average days on market. Know these for the specific neighbourhood you're presenting in — not just the city average.

**List-to-sale ratio:** What sold comps achieved as a percentage of list price. 98% means homes sold for 98 cents on the dollar. 103% means bidding wars. Under 95% means price reductions are common. This tells you how hot or soft the sub-market is.

**Days on market:** The average time from list to accepted offer in the neighbourhood. Low DOM = competitive market, price accordingly. High DOM = buyers have leverage, overpricing will sit. Present this to sellers as context, not as a warning.

**How to present these numbers to a seller:**

*"What I'm seeing in your neighbourhood right now is that homes are selling at about [X]% of list price, and the average time to an offer is [Y] days. That tells me the market here is [active / balanced / softer than the city average]. Which is actually good news for us — because it means if we price this right, we know exactly what to expect."*

Present these as tools for their decision-making, not as pressure. The seller should feel informed, not cornered.

- Pull list-to-sale ratio and DOM from your sold comps — MLS Matrix calculates both automatically in the stats summary view
- Use the last 90 days in the same area and property type — not the full city report
- If the neighbourhood has fewer than 5 sold comps in 90 days, widen your radius or extend to 6 months and note the caveat
- If numbers are shifting quickly (rising or falling market), note the trend — don't just show a static average

## What to Bring to the Appointment — Physical and Digital

What you bring to the appointment signals how seriously you take the opportunity. A printed CMA in a folder reads differently than a screenshot on your phone. Show up like the professional you are.

**Print and bring:**

- Printed CMA — active, sold, and expired comps with your pricing recommendation clearly marked
- Net proceeds estimate — what the seller walks away with at your recommended price, after commission and closing costs
- Your listing presentation (ask Mitch for the team version if you don't have one yet)
- Recent sales one-pager for the neighbourhood — 3–5 relevant sold comps they can hold in their hand
- Your business card — always, even if they already have it

**Have on your phone / tablet:**

- MLS access — be ready to pull listings live if the conversation goes somewhere unexpected
- Your listing marketing examples — past listing photos, social posts, virtual tours you've done or the team has done
- Google Maps / Street View — useful for referencing nearby comps in real time
- A calculator — for adjusting the net proceeds estimate on the spot if they push on price or commission
- FUB — so you can log notes and next steps immediately after

> ✅ **The net proceeds sheet is underused and underrated**
Most sellers are not thinking about gross sale price — they're thinking about what lands in their bank account. A net proceeds estimate that shows them exactly what they walk away with at three different price points ($X, $X–10k, $X+10k) reframes the entire pricing conversation. It's also your best tool when they push for a higher list price — show them the net difference is smaller than they think.

## How to Prepare Mentally for the Pricing Conversation

The pricing conversation is where listing appointments are won or lost — and where new agents feel the most pressure. The seller almost always wants more than the market supports. Your job is not to cave and not to fight. It's to educate, anchor, and hold your recommendation with confidence.

Before you walk in, know your number. Not a range. A number. Agents who present a range give sellers permission to choose the high end every time. Present a specific recommended list price with a rationale — then be ready to discuss it, not defend it.

> 🚨 **The trap: buying the listing**
"Buying the listing" means telling a seller what they want to hear — agreeing to a higher price than the market supports just to get the contract signed. It feels like a win in the room. It always ends badly: price reductions, days on market stacking up, a frustrated seller, and a damaged relationship. Don't do it. A listing priced right is worth more than a listing priced high.
> 

**When the seller pushes for a higher price:**

*"I completely understand — and I want to get you every dollar this home is worth. The number I'm recommending isn't conservative to protect myself. It's the number the market is telling us buyers will pay. If we go higher, we risk sitting — and a home that sits loses leverage. Can I show you what's happened to the homes in this area that listed above market?"*

Then show the expired listings. Let the data make the case. You're not the one saying no — the market is.

- Know your CMA cold before you walk in — you should be able to discuss any comp without looking at the paper
- Anticipate the "our neighbour got more" objection and have the specific comp pulled up and ready
- Decide in advance: what is your walk-away price? If a seller insists on listing at a number you don't believe in, it is okay to say no. Discuss with Mitch before you decline a listing.
- If you feel underprepared on price, call Mitch or Kyle before the appointment — not after

## Pre-Listing Appointment Checklist

### 48 hours before

- [ ]  Pull the full MLS history on the property — every listing, price change, and sale
- [ ]  Note the seller's motivation and timeline (from your initial conversation or intake call)
- [ ]  Research the neighbourhood — recent sales, current actives, expired listings from the last 90 days
- [ ]  Begin building your CMA in MLS Matrix — active, sold, and expired comps
- [ ]  Drive by the property (or do a Google Street View review if distance is an issue)

### 24 hours before

- [ ]  Finalize your CMA and land on a specific recommended list price
- [ ]  Prepare the net proceeds estimate at your recommended price (and two alternates)
- [ ]  Pull list-to-sale ratio and average DOM for the neighbourhood
- [ ]  Print your CMA, net proceeds sheet, and any neighbourhood sold comp summaries
- [ ]  Review your listing presentation — know the key points, don't read from it
- [ ]  Identify the most likely objection and prepare your response (refer to Module 5.3)
- [ ]  If uncertain on any aspect of pricing or strategy — call Mitch

### Day of appointment

- [ ]  Arrive 5–10 minutes early — use the time to walk the exterior and observe the street
- [ ]  Materials in order: CMA, net proceeds, listing presentation, business cards
- [ ]  Phone charged, MLS accessible, FUB open for post-appointment logging
- [ ]  Know your recommended price and your rationale — by heart, not by paper

### After the appointment

- [ ]  Log notes in FUB immediately — what was discussed, what their concerns were, next steps
- [ ]  Send a follow-up email within 2 hours confirming what was discussed and next steps
- [ ]  If they didn't sign — set a follow-up task for 48 hours out
- [ ]  Debrief with Mitch or Kyle on any appointment where you didn't get the listing

> 💡 **Debrief every listing appointment you don't win**
The ones you lose teach you more than the ones you win. If a seller chose another agent, ask Mitch to help you understand why — was it price, presentation, confidence, or something else? One honest debrief per lost listing will accelerate your development faster than any course.
