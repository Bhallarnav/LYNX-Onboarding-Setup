Going live on MLS is a moment you only get once with each listing. The first 72 hours drive more traffic — and more offers — than any other point in the listing lifecycle. Get the launch right.

---

## MLS Input — Fields That Matter, Remarks That Sell, Common Mistakes

Every field in MLS Matrix feeds a buyer search filter somewhere. Incomplete or inaccurate data doesn't just look sloppy — it literally hides the listing from buyers who should be seeing it. Fill every field. Check every field.

| Field | What to Know |
| --- | --- |
| List price | Entered exactly as agreed and confirmed with the seller in writing before input. Even a $1,000 typo is a problem. Double-check before saving. |
| Property type | Get this right — detached, semi-detached, townhouse, condo apartment. Wrong type routes the listing into the wrong search results entirely. |
| Square footage | Use RMS (Residential Measurement Standard) in Alberta — above-grade only unless otherwise noted. Never estimate. Pull from the title or RPR. If you're unsure, ask Mitch. |
| Bedrooms / bathrooms | Count accurately. A basement bedroom that doesn't meet egress requirements doesn't count as a legal bedroom. When in doubt, note it as a den or bonus room. |
| Year built | Pull from the title or RPR — don't guess. Buyers filter on this field and it affects insurance, inspection expectations, and negotiation. |
| Lot size | From the RPR or title. For infills or irregular lots, note the shape in remarks if it's unusual. |
| Possession date | Confirm with the seller. "Immediate," "negotiable," and a specific date mean different things to buyers. Be precise. |
| Inclusions / exclusions | List every included appliance. If the seller is keeping a specific fixture or appliance, it goes in exclusions — not just left blank. This field prevents disputes at possession. |
| Public remarks | The most important text field in the listing. See the remarks section below — don't rush this. |

> ✅ **Writing public remarks that actually sell**
Your remarks are the first thing a buyer reads after seeing the photos. They have 30 seconds of attention. Don't waste it on the address or "beautiful home in great neighbourhood." Lead with the single best thing about this property — the view, the renovation, the location, the lot. Then layer in the next two or three strongest features. Close with a reason to act: the lifestyle this home enables, the neighbourhood it's in, or simply that it won't last. Write it like a human, not a checklist.
> 

**What good remarks look like:**

*"Backing directly onto Nose Hill Park with no rear neighbours — this is the one people wait years to find in Brentwood. Fully renovated main floor (2022) with an open kitchen, quartz counters, and a mudroom that actually works. Upstairs has three generous bedrooms including a primary with a walk-in and full ensuite. The backyard is the real story — large deck, mature trees, complete privacy. Walking distance to schools, the LRT, and the off-leash. Possession flexible."*

Notice what's not here: "Don't miss out," "gorgeous home," "shows 10/10," or any filler phrases. Every sentence earns its place.

**Common MLS mistakes:**

| What agents write | What it should say |
| --- | --- |
| "Beautiful home in great neighbourhood, shows 10/10, don't miss out!" | Open with the home's single strongest feature — location, view, renovation, or lot. Make it specific. |
| "Hardwood floors throughout, granite counters, stainless steel appliances" | Features without context are noise. Tie them to benefit: "Refinished hardwood flows through the main floor, giving the space a warmth that shows immediately." |
| Leaving fields like "possession" or "inclusions" blank | Fill every field. Blank fields create friction — agents call to ask, buyers wonder if something's wrong, search filters exclude the listing. |
| Entering square footage from memory or a listing sheet without verifying | Always pull from the RPR or title. Wrong square footage is a liability — and buyers notice when the space doesn't match. |

## Photo Day — How to Prepare the Seller and What to Watch For

Photos are the listing. Before a buyer books a showing, they've already decided whether they're interested based entirely on your photography. A poorly prepared home photographed badly will get fewer showings — guaranteed. Protect the launch by owning photo day preparation.

**Tell the seller to do:**

- Declutter every surface — counters, tables, shelves. If it's not decorative, it goes in a drawer or a box
- Remove personal photos from walls and surfaces — buyers need to picture themselves, not your family
- Clear the garage and driveway of vehicles the day of the shoot
- Make all beds, close all toilet lids, hang fresh towels in bathrooms
- Put away pet beds, food bowls, and toys
- Turn on every light in the house — overhead, lamps, under-cabinet, everything
- Mow the lawn, tidy the front entrance, remove any outdoor clutter

**Your job on photo day:**

- Arrive before the photographer — do a walkthrough and fix anything the seller missed
- Open all blinds and curtains for maximum natural light
- Remove anything that shouldn't be in the frame — remotes, cords, cleaning products, garbage cans
- Watch for things the photographer might miss: the corner of a cluttered room, a half-open closet door, a distracting item in a reflection
- Make sure the exterior shots happen — they're often the first photo a buyer sees
- Review the photos before the photographer leaves if possible — it's much easier to reshoot on the day than return

> ⚠️ **Send the photo prep guide at least 48 hours in advance**
Sellers underestimate how much work photo prep takes. A quick walkthrough the morning of isn't enough. Send written instructions two days before — and offer to do a prep walkthrough with them the evening before if the home needs it. A seller who says "the house is always clean" almost always needs this reminder.

## Marketing Checklist — Where the Listing Gets Promoted

Going live on MLS is the floor, not the ceiling. A well-launched listing gets promoted across every channel simultaneously — the first 48 hours of exposure drives the most activity. Don't stagger this. Launch everything at once.

| Task | Owner |
| --- | --- |
| MLS listing live — all fields complete, photos uploaded in correct order, remarks finalized | You |
| Realtor.ca verified — confirm the listing synced correctly and photos are displaying | You |
| Instagram post + story — listing photos with a strong hook caption. Use the team Canva template. | You + Mitch review |
| Facebook post — same content adapted for Facebook format and audience | You |
| Feature sheets printed — ready for showings and any physical marketing | You |
| For sale sign installed — LYNX sign with your name rider, positioned for maximum visibility | You / coordinate with team |
| Agent outreach — personally contact 5–10 agents with active buyers in this neighbourhood and price range | You |
| Sphere text blast — "Just listed a great property in [neighbourhood] — know anyone looking?" | You |
| Lockbox installed and access confirmed — test it yourself before the first showing | You |
| Showing instructions set — confirmed with seller, entered correctly in MLS and ShowingTime | You |

> 📋 **Canva templates — use them, don't improvise**
The team has Canva templates for listing posts, stories, and feature sheets. They're built to LYNX brand standards and they look professional. Don't create your own from scratch — use the template, swap in the photos and details, get Mitch's eyes on it before it goes live. Ask Kyle for access to the shared Canva folder if you don't have it yet.

## Setting Seller Expectations at Launch

A seller who doesn't know what to expect will fill the silence with anxiety. Before you launch, have an explicit conversation about what the first week looks like — how showings work, what feedback sounds like, and when they'll hear from you. Managing expectations upfront prevents 80% of the difficult conversations that happen later.

- Showings are booked through ShowingTime — the seller will get a notification and needs to confirm or decline within the window you've set. Walk them through the app before launch.
- Not every showing generates feedback. Buyer agents don't always respond. This is normal — it doesn't mean there's a problem.
- Early showing activity is a signal, not a guarantee. A lot of showings in week one is great. No offer yet doesn't mean something's wrong.
- They need to be out of the home — with pets — for every showing. No exceptions. Buyers don't talk freely when the seller is present.
- The home needs to be showing-ready at all times.
- You will call them at the end of every week — no news is not a reason to go dark.

**What to say before launch:**

*"Before we go live, I want to make sure we're aligned on what the first week looks like. You're going to start getting showing requests — sometimes with short notice. The more flexible you can be with access, the better. After each showing I'll follow up with the buyer's agent for feedback, and I'll send you a summary. You'll hear from me every week regardless of what's happening. And if anything shifts — market activity, feedback themes, anything that changes our strategy — I'll call you directly. Any questions before we launch?"*

This conversation takes five minutes and prevents weeks of anxiety calls. Don't skip it.

## How to Communicate With the Seller in the First 72 Hours

The first 72 hours are the most emotionally charged period of a listing. The seller is watching their phone, counting showings, and trying to interpret every silence. Your job is to be present, informative, and calm — even when there's nothing to report yet.

---

**Launch day**

Confirm the listing is live, all channels are active, and everything looks right. Send the seller the Realtor.ca link so they can see it themselves.

*"Hey [Name] — you're live! Here's your listing: [link]. Everything looks great. Showings are already being requested — I'll keep you updated as they come in."*

---

**End of Day 1**

Quick update — how many showings are booked, any early feedback if you have it. Even a two-sentence text keeps them from spiralling.

*"Quick update — [X] showings booked so far for the next couple of days. Good early activity. I'll follow up with each agent after and share what I hear."*

---

**After each showing**

Follow up with the buyer's agent within a few hours of the showing. Even if you don't get feedback, you tried — and the seller should know you tried.

*"Hey [Name] — followed up with the agent from today's showing. [Share whatever feedback you got, or: 'Haven't heard back yet — I'll follow up again tomorrow.'] Nothing actionable yet but good to have eyes on it."*

---

**72-hour check-in**

A real call — not a text. By now you have showing data, early feedback themes, and a sense of whether the launch has momentum. Give them an honest picture and a clear next step.

*"Hey [Name], it's [Your Name] — just wanted to do our 72-hour check-in. Here's where we stand: [X] showings completed, [Y] booked coming up. The feedback so far has been [summary]. Based on this, I think we're [on track / need to talk about X]. How are you feeling about everything?"*

> 🚨 **Silence is not a strategy**
The agents who lose seller trust fastest are the ones who go quiet after launch. Your seller is watching every showing notification and wondering why the phone isn't ringing. Proactive communication — even when there's nothing to report — keeps the relationship stable and your credibility intact. If you don't know what's happening, say so. "I'm following up now" is better than nothing.

## Listing Launch Checklist

### Before photo day

- [ ]  Photo prep guide sent to seller at least 48 hours in advance
- [ ]  Photo day confirmed with photographer — time, address, any special instructions
- [ ]  Seller knows to clear the driveway, turn on all lights, and be out for the shoot

### Photo day

- [ ]  Arrive before photographer — do a full walkthrough, fix anything missed
- [ ]  All blinds open, all lights on, all clutter cleared
- [ ]  Exterior photos confirmed — front, backyard, and any notable outdoor features
- [ ]  Review photos before photographer leaves if possible

### MLS input

- [ ]  All fields complete — property type, square footage (RMS), bedrooms, bathrooms, year built, lot size
- [ ]  Inclusions and exclusions confirmed with seller and entered accurately
- [ ]  Possession date confirmed and entered precisely
- [ ]  Public remarks written, reviewed, and finalized — lead with the strongest feature
- [ ]  Photos uploaded in correct order — exterior first, then main living areas, then bedrooms, then secondary spaces
- [ ]  Showing instructions entered and confirmed with seller
- [ ]  Listing reviewed in full before activating — read every field out loud

### Launch day

- [ ]  Listing active on MLS — verify it synced to Realtor.ca correctly
- [ ]  Realtor.ca link sent to seller
- [ ]  Instagram post and story live — Canva template, reviewed by Mitch
- [ ]  Facebook post live
- [ ]  Feature sheets printed and available for showings
- [ ]  For sale sign installed and lockbox tested
- [ ]  Agent network outreach — 5–10 agents with active buyers in the area personally contacted
- [ ]  Sphere text sent to relevant contacts
- [ ]  Seller expectations conversation completed — showings, feedback, communication cadence
- [ ]  End-of-day update sent to seller

### First week

- [ ]  Follow up with every showing agent within 24 hours for feedback
- [ ]  Feedback logged in FUB and shared with seller
- [ ]  72-hour check-in call completed with seller
- [ ]  End-of-week summary sent — showing count, feedback themes, next steps

> 💡 **Your first listing launch — do it with Kyle**
Before you run a listing launch independently, walk through the MLS input and marketing checklist with Kyle present. One missed field or a misaligned photo order can be fixed quickly — but not after 48 hours of traffic has already passed. The first launch is a joint exercise. After that, you own it.
