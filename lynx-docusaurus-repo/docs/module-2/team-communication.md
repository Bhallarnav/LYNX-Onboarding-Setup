### Channel directory — what goes where

:::note
Use the right channel for the right thing. Dumping everything into one channel or DMing Mitch for things that belong in a channel creates noise and slows everyone down.

:::

- Channel breakdown
    - **#general** — team-wide announcements, introductions, anything the whole team should see. Not for questions or deal chatter — keep it clean.
    - **#listings** — active listing updates, showing feedback, open house results, price changes on team listings. If it's about a property we have listed — it goes here.
    - **#deals** — offer updates, accepted deals, condition removals, closings. When your deal goes under contract — post it here. When it closes — post it here. Wins get celebrated in this channel.
    - **#questions** — the place to ask process questions, tech questions, or anything you'd otherwise DM the whole team about. Post here first — someone always knows the answer.
    - **#marketing** — content approvals, social media ideas, Canva questions, anything brand related. Tag Kyle when you need a review.
    - **#general-chat** — casual conversation, memes, life updates, anything off-topic. The team has a personality — use this channel.
    - **Direct messages** — for conversations that are specific to one person and don't benefit the wider team. Use DMs for personal feedback, sensitive client situations, or quick back-and-forths that would clutter a channel.
- A note on channel names
    
    Your team's actual channel names may differ slightly from the above — check what channels exist when you join and ask Kyle if anything isn't clear. The logic above applies regardless of exact names.

### Response time expectations

:::note
Slack is not email. It moves fast. Respond like it's a text message, not a formal letter you'll get to eventually.

:::

- The response time standard
    - **Messages from Mitch or Kyle** — within 1 hour during business hours. If it's urgent, they'll say so — treat it accordingly.
    - **Messages from peer agents** — same day. You don't need to drop everything, but don't let a teammate wait until tomorrow for a quick answer.
    - **#questions channel** — if you know the answer, jump in. Don't wait for someone else to answer. A team that helps each other is faster than one where everyone waits on management.
    - **DMs** — treat like a text. Respond when you see it, within reason.
- If you're going to be unavailable
    
    Post a heads up in #general or set your Slack status. Something simple: *"In appointments all day — back online after 4pm."* Takes 10 seconds and saves three people from wondering why you're not responding. If you're away for a full day during a live deal — make sure your client knows who to contact in your absence.

### How to ask a good question in Slack

:::note
The quality of the question determines the quality of the answer. A vague question gets a vague answer or no answer at all. A specific question gets solved in one message.

:::

- The formula for a good Slack question
    - **Context** — what's the situation?
    - **What you've already tried** — what did you look up or attempt first?
    - **The specific question** — what exactly do you need?
    - Bad: *"How do I handle conditions?"*
    - Good: *"I have a buyer offer going in tonight on a detached in Airdrie. They want a financing condition and an inspection condition. I've looked at the AREA contract in Module 3.4 — what's the standard wording we use at Lynx for conditions and what timeframe do we typically recommend?"*
    
    The second question gets answered immediately and completely. The first one starts a back-and-forth that wastes everyone's time.
    
- Use threads — don't clutter channels
    
    When someone posts in a channel and you're responding to that specific message — reply in the thread, not as a new message in the channel. Click the speech bubble icon on the original message to open a thread. This keeps channels readable and conversations organized.
    
- Tag people when you need them specifically
    
    Use @name when a message needs a specific person's attention. Use @channel sparingly — only when something genuinely needs everyone to see it immediately. Don't @channel for things that can wait. Don't forget to tag someone when their input is actually needed — messages without tags get missed.

### When Slack isn't enough — pick up the phone

:::note
Slack is fast for simple things. For anything complex, urgent, or emotionally charged — a 2-minute call beats a 20-message thread every time.

:::

- Call instead of Slack when
    - A client is upset or a deal is at risk
    - You need an answer in the next 15 minutes
    - The conversation has already gone back and forth 3+ times in Slack
    - You're discussing something sensitive about a client or a deal
    - It involves a contract clause, compliance question, or negotiation decision
    - You made a mistake and need to tell Mitch — call, don't Slack
- The rule of thumb
    
    If you're typing a Slack message that's more than 4–5 lines long — it should probably be a call. Long Slack messages are hard to act on and easy to misread. Pick up the phone.

### Notification settings — what to have on

:::note
Notifications are a balance — too many and you're constantly interrupted, too few and you miss things that matter.

:::

- Recommended settings
    
    **During business hours:**
    
    - All direct messages — on
    - @mentions — on
    - #deals and #listings — on (these move fast and you need to stay current)
    - Other channels — activity only or off
    
    **After hours:**
    
    - DMs from Mitch — on (set as an exception if you use Do Not Disturb)
    - Everything else — off. You're allowed to be offline.
    
    Set up Do Not Disturb in Slack for your off hours — it still allows Mitch or Kyle to override if something is genuinely urgent.

### Team communication — the bigger picture

:::note
The way you communicate inside the team is a direct preview of how you communicate with clients. Agents who are responsive, clear, and helpful in Slack tend to be the same way with the people they represent. Agents who go quiet, give vague updates, and let things sit — do the same thing to their clients.

Communication is a skill. Treat Slack like a place to practice it.

:::
